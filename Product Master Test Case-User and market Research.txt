30 Test Cases for Product Manager Agent Testing
Overview
This test suite evaluates an AI Agent designed to assist product managers with market and user research across 5 product categories: AI Agent, Enterprise Internal Software, Hardware Product, Software Tools (SaaS), and Consumer Mobile Apps. Each test case includes realistic user queries, standard answers, and a detailed 4-dimensional scoring rubric.

Scoring Rubric Framework
Each test case is evaluated across 4 dimensions, each scored out of 10 points (40 points total):
Dimension A: User Requirements (10 points)
10 points: Accurately identifies all explicit and implicit user needs; demonstrates deep understanding of the underlying business problem; correctly interprets technical and non-technical requirements
7 points: Identifies most user needs; shows good understanding of the core problem; minor gaps in interpreting implicit requirements
4 points: Identifies only surface-level needs; partial understanding of the problem; misses key implicit requirements
1 point: Fails to identify user needs accurately; misinterprets the core problem; focuses on irrelevant aspects
Dimension B: Target Users (10 points)
10 points: Precisely defines core user segments with demographic/psychographic details; identifies 3+ critical unmet needs with supporting evidence; explains pain point severity and frequency
7 points: Identifies main user segments with reasonable detail; identifies 2 critical unmet needs; explains pain points with some evidence
4 points: Generic user segment definition; identifies 1 unmet need; vague pain point description
1 point: Incorrect or no user segment identification; fails to identify unmet needs; no pain point analysis
Dimension C: Market Analysis (10 points)
10 points: Identifies 5+ relevant competitors with accurate positioning; provides market share data and differentiation analysis; includes emerging competitors and market gaps
7 points: Identifies 3-4 competitors with positioning; provides some market data; mentions key differentiators
4 points: Lists 1-2 competitors with minimal detail; lacks market data; superficial differentiation
1 point: No competitors identified or completely irrelevant competitors; no market understanding
Dimension D: Market Insights (10 points)
10 points: Provides 3+ actionable positioning recommendations with clear rationale; identifies specific market opportunities with TAM/SAM data; suggests differentiation strategies tied to user needs
7 points: Provides 2 positioning recommendations; identifies market opportunities with general sizing; suggests differentiation strategies
4 points: Provides 1 vague recommendation; mentions opportunities without sizing; generic strategies
1 point: No actionable insights; irrelevant recommendations; lacks strategic thinking

Test Case 5.5: Social Fitness App
Test Case Type: Consumer Mobile App
User Question: "Fitness apps often lack social features beyond leaderboards. What social mechanics would actually motivate users to work out more consistently?"
Standard Answer:
User Requirements Analysis: The user needs to understand limitations of current fitness app social features, identify what social mechanics drive behavior change (not just engagement), learn from gaming/social app psychology, and understand accountability vs competition dynamics. Implicit needs include understanding workout tracking data structure, privacy concerns in fitness sharing, and monetization of social features.
Target Users:
Primary: Fitness beginners/intermediates aged 25-40 who struggle with consistency and need external motivation
Secondary: People who lost workout partners post-pandemic, parents with limited gym time
Critical Unmet Needs:
a.Accountability partnerships—not leaderboards with strangers, but paired commitment with friend/family where both parties check in on each other
b.Micro-social interactions—current apps require posting full workout; users want quick "finished my run!" with emoji reactions, not social media pressure
c.Group challenges with meaningful stakes—leaderboards don't motivate; need team-based challenges where letting down the team matters
Market Competitors:
1.Strava - 100M+ users, freemium + $11.99/month, athlete social network, segment leaderboards, cycling/running focus
2.Peloton App - 6.6M+ subscribers, $12.99/month, classes + social features, declining from pandemic peak
3.Nike Run Club - 100M+ downloads, free, run tracking + challenges, Nike ecosystem
4.MapMyRun (Under Armour) - 60M+ users, freemium + $5.99/month, route tracking, casual runner focus
5.Fitbit Premium - Part of Fitbit, $9.99/month, device data + challenges, Google ecosystem
Market Insights: Social features can drive retention but must align with user psychology. Effective social mechanics:
1.Accountability pods (2-4 people): Let users form small pods with commitments ("work out 3×/week"); pod members get notifications if someone misses goal—intimate accountability vs public leaderboard
2.Asynchronous challenges: Not "who runs fastest" but "complete these 5 workouts this week"—team succeeds together, accommodates different fitness levels and schedules
3.Celebration over competition: Default to celebrating others' workouts (easy emoji reactions) rather than comparing performance—reduce intimidation for beginners
4.Privacy-first sharing: Let users share "I worked out" without revealing specific metrics unless they choose—many users don't want to share weight, pace, etc.
5.Monetization: Free social features + $9.99/month for AI-generated workout plans and nutrition guidance
6.Market positioning: 150M+ people with gym memberships/fitness intent × 15% app adoption × $80 ARPU = $1.8B TAM for social fitness apps

Test Case 5.6: Habit Tracking App
Test Case Type: Consumer Mobile App
User Question: "Dozens of habit tracking apps exist. What causes users to abandon them, and how can a new app help users actually form lasting habits?"
Standard Answer:
User Requirements Analysis: The user needs to understand the gap between tracking habits and forming habits, identify abandonment patterns (when and why), learn what behavioral science says about habit formation, and differentiate between features and outcomes. Implicit needs include understanding streak psychology, notification fatigue, and when apps should "graduate" users.
Target Users:
Primary: Self-improvement enthusiasts aged 25-40 who've tried 2-3 habit apps before but stopped using them
Secondary: People with ADHD/executive function challenges who need external structure
Critical Unmet Needs:
a.Habit stacking and triggers—apps track individual habits in isolation; don't help users attach new habits to existing routines ("after coffee, meditate")
b.Gradual difficulty progression—users try to meditate 30 min/day on day 1 and fail; need smart ramping (start with 2 min, increase 1 min/week)
c.Identity reinforcement over tracking—apps focus on "you did it!" not "you're becoming a person who does this"—identity shift is key to lasting change
Market Competitors:
1.Habitica - 8M+ users, freemium + $4.99/month, gamification via RPG mechanics, quest-based
2.Streaks - $4.99 one-time purchase, iOS-only, 24-habit limit, simple design, no subscription
3.Habitify - 2M+ downloads, free + $4.99/month, routine focus, time-based habits
4.Loop Habit Tracker - Open source, free, Android-only, simple tracking, privacy-focused
5.Done - Freemium + $2.99/month, streak-focused, minimal design, Apple platform exclusive
Market Insights: Habit apps have high initial adoption but 90% abandon within 3 months. Lasting habit formation strategies:
1.Implementation intentions: Force users to define "when/where/how" not just "what"—research shows implementation intentions 2-3× effectiveness
2.Adaptive coaching: Use AI to detect when user is struggling (3 missed days) and suggest making habit easier or changing trigger time—not just "try harder"
3.Identity milestones: After 30 days of meditation, unlock "meditator" identity badge and shift language from "do meditation" to "you're a meditator"—self-perception shift
4.Graduation mechanism: After 90 days of successful habit, offer to "graduate" habit to autopilot and free up focus for new habit—signal success
5.Community accountability: Small groups (5-10 people) working on same habit category with weekly reflection threads
6.Pricing: Free for 3 habits, $6.99/month unlimited + coaching features
7.Market sizing: 200M+ people interested in habit formation globally × 5% paid adoption × $60 ARPU = $600M TAM
Scoring Rubric:
User Requirements (10 pts): 10 = Distinguishes tracking from forming, identifies abandonment patterns, applies behavioral science, features vs outcomes, streak psychology, notification fatigue, graduation concept; 7 = Abandonment and science; 4 = "Better reminders"; 1 = Focuses only on tracking
Target Users (10 pts): 10 = Age, prior app usage, ADHD segment, personas, 3 unmet needs with stacking/progression/identity specifics; 7 = Profile and 2 needs; 4 = "Self-improvement people" with 1 need; 1 = Wrong target
Market Analysis (10 pts): 10 = Lists 5 competitors with users/pricing/core mechanic and abandonment context; 7 = Lists 3-4 with data; 4 = Lists 2 apps; 1 = No competitors
Market Insights (10 pts): 10 = Implementation intentions, adaptive coaching, identity milestones, graduation, community, pricing, TAM sizing; 7 = Four strategies; 4 = Generic; 1 = No insights
